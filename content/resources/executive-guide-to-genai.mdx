## What Is Generative AI, Actually?

Generative AI refers to a class of AI models that can **create new content** — text, code, images, audio, video, and more — in response to a prompt. The most prominent examples are large language models (LLMs) like GPT-4, Claude, and Gemini.

What makes these models remarkable is that they've been trained on vast amounts of human-generated content, which gives them the ability to:

- Write, summarize, and edit text at high quality
- Answer questions by reasoning across information
- Generate working code from natural language descriptions
- Extract structured data from unstructured documents
- Translate, classify, and transform content at scale

They are **not** databases. They don't "look things up." They predict what a helpful response would look like based on patterns learned during training — which is why they can occasionally be confidently wrong (the "hallucination" problem we'll address below).

## What GenAI Is Good At (And Where It Struggles)

### Strong Use Cases

| Task | Example |
|------|---------|
| Content drafting | First drafts of reports, proposals, emails, marketing copy |
| Summarization | Condensing lengthy documents, call transcripts, research |
| Q&A over documents | Ask questions of a policy manual, contracts, or knowledge base |
| Code generation | Writing scripts, automating workflows, generating test cases |
| Customer-facing chatbots | Answering FAQs, triage, support deflection |
| Data extraction | Pulling structured data from unstructured documents |

### Where Leaders Should Be Cautious

**Hallucinations:** GenAI models can generate plausible-sounding but incorrect information — including fake citations, wrong numbers, and fabricated facts. Always verify high-stakes outputs.

**Sensitive data:** Sending proprietary or regulated data to third-party AI systems creates legal and compliance risk. Understand what your vendors do with inputs.

**Consistency:** AI outputs vary. For high-stakes decisions (legal, medical, financial), human review is essential.

**Bias:** Models reflect the data they were trained on and can amplify societal biases. Applications affecting people's lives need careful testing.

## The Real Business Opportunity

The value of Generative AI for most organizations isn't replacing workers — it's **dramatically reducing the time to produce quality work**. Consider:

- A team that produces 5 market analyses per quarter could produce 25 — same headcount
- Customer support that handles 200 tickets per day could handle 800
- A software team spending 40% of time on documentation could cut that to 10%

The unlocks are in **leverage**: doing more with the same people, or redirecting human effort to higher-value work.

Research from McKinsey estimates GenAI could add $2.6–4.4 trillion in annual value to the global economy. The bulk of this value sits in knowledge work — exactly where most executive teams spend their time.

## 5 Questions Every Leader Should Be Asking in 2025

### 1. What's our AI governance posture?

Right now, employees at most organizations are already using ChatGPT, Copilot, and other AI tools — whether IT knows it or not. The question isn't whether AI is entering your organization. It's whether you're managing it.

**Action:** Establish a clear AI use policy. Define what tools are sanctioned, what data can be used with them, and who is accountable.

### 2. What are our top 3 GenAI use cases, and what's the ROI case?

Not every AI use case is worth pursuing. The best early wins are typically high-volume, repetitive knowledge tasks where quality variation is tolerable and time savings are measurable.

**Action:** Run a structured use case identification exercise. Score candidates against impact, feasibility, risk, and data readiness.

### 3. What are the risks specific to our industry and regulatory environment?

Financial services, healthcare, legal, and government organizations face specific AI regulations — from EU AI Act compliance to sector-specific guidance from regulators. Even if you're not in a regulated industry, IP, privacy, and reputational risk are real.

**Action:** Assign ownership of AI risk to a specific leader. Commission a risk assessment.

### 4. Are we building internal AI capability or staying dependent on vendors?

There are two viable strategies: **buy** (SaaS AI tools, vendor-managed solutions) and **build** (fine-tuned models, custom pipelines, in-house AI teams). Most organizations need a hybrid. Dependency on a single vendor is a strategic risk.

**Action:** Define your AI build-vs-buy philosophy before committing to large vendor contracts.

### 5. How are we preparing our people?

The biggest barrier to successful AI adoption isn't technology — it's people. Leaders who communicate clearly, invest in upskilling, and model AI literacy signal that the organization is moving forward together.

**Action:** Invest in AI literacy programs at every level. Start with leaders.
