The deal isn't the hard part.

Everyone obsesses over the LOI, the multiple, the terms. And yes — those matter.

But the moment the funds wire and the keys change hands, you realize the transaction was the easy part.

What comes next is where acquisitions either succeed or quietly fall apart.

Before my first acquisition, I treated due diligence as a financial exercise. Revenue, EBITDA, customer concentration, contracts. I ran the numbers clean.

What I didn't fully appreciate? I was also buying a culture, a set of unwritten rules, and someone's life work.

That last part changes everything about how you walk in on day one.

Standard due diligence won't tell you that the top salesperson only stays because of her relationship with the founder. It won't reveal that the ops team has been running on loyalty and fear in roughly equal measure.

You're not just buying a business. You're inheriting a community — and they're watching every move you make.

The changes you make in the first ninety days will define how that team sees you for years.

Make sure they're the right ones.

I wrote about everything I wish I'd known before closing that first deal — the financial gaps, the emotional weight, and what a real integration plan actually requires.

LINKEDIN — POST 2 (Insight Angle)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Most acquirers fail at integration before they ever make a single change.

They walk in with a 90-day plan built entirely on what the financials told them.

But the financials can't tell you that the ops team ran on loyalty and fear in equal measure. They can't show you that the founder promised his team nothing would change — because he needed to believe that too.

Here's the counterintuitive truth: the best acquirers move slower in the first 90 days, not faster.

They do a cultural audit before close — talking to employees at every level, not just leadership. They listen for what isn't said. They map the gap between the org chart and how decisions actually get made.

Because that gap? That's where your integration plan will either hold or crack.

Earning trust before earning the right to change things isn't a soft strategy. It's the actual strategy.
