Good intentions don't scale.

Most philanthropists start the same way — something moves them, and they write a check. That impulse is genuine. But impulse-driven giving rarely compounds.

The need continues. The system that created the need stays exactly where it was.

There's a shift that separates philanthropy that lasts from philanthropy that fades by March — and it starts with thinking like an investor, not a donor.

Strategic philanthropy asks a harder question than 'how much?' It asks why the problem persists — and then funds the answer.

That means looking upstream. Multi-year commitments over one-time gifts. Funding operations, not just programs. Using your network and your voice alongside your capital.

At the Ayana Foundation, we didn't start by asking what we wanted to fund. We started by listening — to communities, to data, to the people already doing the work without enough support.

Strategy without proximity is just theory.

The gap between writing a check and building a system is exactly where lasting change is made or missed.

Here's the question most philanthropists never ask:

What would $50,000 unlock that $500,000 going elsewhere wouldn't?

That's not a trick question. It's the difference between strategic giving and reflexive giving.

When funding is already concentrated in certain organizations or geographies, adding more to those pools rarely moves the needle. The real leverage is often in the gaps — the leaders doing extraordinary work without enough infrastructure, the policy initiatives that could restructure entire systems but can't get in the room.

One of the most overlooked forms of generosity isn't money at all. It's access. A relationship. A room someone couldn't otherwise enter.

The most effective givers I know treat their network as part of their philanthropic portfolio — not a bonus, but a core asset.

If your giving strategy doesn't include that question — where is the real gap, and what do I uniquely have to offer there — you may be funding comfort more than change.
